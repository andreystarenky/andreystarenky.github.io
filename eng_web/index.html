Family and
Education

This section will outline Shakespeare's close family and education, from parents and siblings to his spouse and children as well as his education throughout his childhood

Home and Early Family

Shakespeare was the first of the children of John Shakespeare and Mary Arden to live past a year, and he would have 5 surviving siblings (although one died at 8 years). While no records of Shakespeare’s education have survived, it is presumed that he attended King Edward VI School in Stratford upon Avon. He was educated in grammar, as math and science knowledge was limited. It is presumed that he had to leave school at 13 to help his family’s financial situation. While the Shakespeares had been financially well off, they landed in debt for unknown reasons. It was serious enough that it prompted John Shakespeare to mortgage his wife’s inheritance.

Father

It is a common misconception that the Shakespeares were not well off. In fact, Mary Arden was the daughter of a wealthy landowner, and John Shakespeare made a good living as a glover and trader of produce. His father even held a position as the equivalent of the mayor of the town (called a high bailiff) in 1569, when Shakespeare was 6 years old.

Spouse

MShakespeare married his wife, Anne Hathaway at 18. This was considered a shotgun wedding as their first daughter Susanna was born 6 months later. A day before official records of marriage to Anne Hathaway, there was a license announcing his marriage to “Anne Whately.” The theories on this wrong name range from being a clerical error, to Shakespeare’s intent to marry another, but being forced to marry Anne Hathaway because he had gotten her pregnant.

Marraige

There are few records as to how happy Shakespeare was with his wife. It is often noted that when he moved to London to pursue his career in the city, his wife and children stayed in his home in Stratford in lieu of accompanying him. We also have the text of his will bequeathing Anne only his second best bed. Leaving a bed in the will was not odd at the time, as the “best” bed was a considerable establishment of the estate that goes to the main heir, which was his daughter Susanna. The second-best bed would likely have been the marital bed, and Shakespeare leaving this for Anne has been interpreted to dozens of different conclusions about the state of their marriage.

Children

Shakespeare and Anne had three children: Susanna, born possibly out of wedlock, and the twins Hamnet and Judith two years later. Hamnet would die at the age of 11 of unknown reasons, commonly assumed to be of the plague. None of Shakespeare’s children had grandchildren, so his direct lineage died out before the end of the 17th century. It is still possible to claim a relationship to Shakespeare’s sister, however, who has numerous descendants living today.

Early Career

This section will cover Shakespeare's early career, from first plays to his acting company, and printed works.

First Plays

Shakespeare's first 3 plays were Henry VI (Parts 1, 2, 3), Two Gentlemen of Verona, and Titus Andronicus. Released in 1592, it is commonly debated which of the 3 works was released first. None of the 3 plays were printed at the time, due to the high costs.

Printed Works

As Shakespeare's works gained popularity, he began to publish his works. His first 2 published works were poems: "Venus and Adonis" - 1593 and "Rape of Lucrece" in 1954. Venus and Adonis features the relation between Venus, the goddess of love, as well as her seduction of Adonis, a handsome young man. The Rape of Lucrece was about the Roman Noblewoman Lucretia. Both poems are comic, erotic, and tragic throughout.

Acting Company

Shakespeare as well as a few actors from various companies came together and combined efforts to form an acting company called Lord Chamberlain's Men. The company was under the patronage of Lord Chamberlain, officer of the Royal Household. Richard Burbage was the main actor, playing lead roles in plays such as Hamlet, Othello, and Macbeth.

Career Continued

Shakespeare produced an average of 2 plays every year, and helped in the overal growth of the company. The Chamberlain's Men would go on to become the King's Men under the patronage of King James I. Throughout his career and became considerably wealthy, and in 1957 he purchased the New Place (pictured on the right), which was the largest house in the area of Stratford-upon-Avon.
